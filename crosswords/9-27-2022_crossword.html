Peter Koetters notes:

I sat frozen with bated breath as the months marched by. Will Shortz and friends clearly were fawning over my previous puzzle submission — a masterpiece worthy of a genius grant featuring the names of the Three Stooges hidden in feminine hygiene brands — and must have forgotten in their reverie to get back to me. I decided to commemorate the hiatus by constructing a puzzle based on the calendar.

I hate our Gregorian calendar. It is an illogical, maddening relic that only gives the impression of symmetry. Hmm. Crossword puzzle, anyone? Well, unless you can convince the UN to consider calendar reform, please suffer through my roughly symmetric jumble of months. Oh, and don't blame me for the ugly shaded parts. They call it "editorial license," whatever that means. Please use the same thought process to apply to any clues you dislike.

Jeff Chen notes:
The MONTHs of the year laid out in such orderly fashion — four rows of three months, approximately spring, summer, fall, and winter. ... read more

The MONTHs of the year laid out in such orderly fashion — four rows of three months, approximately spring, summer, fall, and winter. Almost perfectly seasoned!

Hey, stop peppering me with hate mail! Why so salty?

Most crosswords playing on the calendar have oriented the trigrams horizontally, and in a mish-mashy way — it's tough to jam twelve of anything into a 15x15 space. Kudos to Peter for doing it so tidily.

It'd be great if the second and third rows weren't overlapping — JUN connected to SEP is a bit odd — but that's a minor detail, especially considering all the factors contributing to the layout difficulty.

Take the bottom row, for instance. It would have been great to push OCT NOV DEC down a row or two for better spacing, but there's not much that ends in OCT besides CONCOCT. Maybe Peter USTINOV is upset not to make a cameo, but since nothing solid ends in DEC, it is what it is.

I'm glad Will Shortz allowed Peter to go to 82 words. I felt like my solve was somewhat start-and-stop, with so many short entries, but a smooth solving experience is the bigger priority. Although there was a lot of EFT ENS EXO etc., it would have been much worse in a 78-word layout.

Calendar puzzles with shading are too easy to fill right immediately — get JAN and FEB and you can mindlessly fill in the rest of the puzzle — so I'm glad Peter managed to work in a couple of solid bonuses like SAILFISH, MEAN JOE Greene, and STOOLIE to keep up my interest.
