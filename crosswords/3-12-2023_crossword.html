David Tuffs, of Pacific Grove, Calif., is a master's student in Linguistics at the University of California Santa Cruz. He's writing a thesis on negative verb forms in Mixtec, an Indigenous language in Mexico. To find potential theme answers for this puzzle, he used Java and a database of English phrases, then worked to get the most elegant examples. This is David's sixth puzzle, and second Sunday, for The Times.

Excited to have another Sunday crossword in the New York Times! Hopefully having circled words AND shaded words in the theme entries was a nice Sunday bonus and avoided turning into too much of a mosaic. While none of the eventual theme entries had either word in one piece, my favorite entry that didn't make it in did: PRIM and PROPER in (PRIM)E (PROPER)TY, which had to be cut because ‘proper' and ‘property' rather expectedly share an etymology.

The final draft of the puzzle was partly assembled in Google Slides, which for me is a return to my roots, as Slides is where I first started making puzzles. However this time I only used it to get the circles and shading right, and not to try and fit the year BC IIII into a 78-word themeless.

Awesome find in BACK and FORTH within BLACK FOREST HAM. As if the meats weren't tasty enough! It's impressive enough to find two halves of a common "X and Y" phrase within another phrase, but to have them so neatly arranged? I'm YODELing over this find.

There are a ton of "X and Y" phrases out there. Finding both components in a single phrase is another story. Add the constraint that they must both be found in proper letter order — and that the X word must start first? It's a wonder that David found so many. All these layers makes me appreciate the BLACK FOREST HAM discovery even more.

Some editors are hesitant to take themes that require circles spread out through themers, saying that they look ungainly and random. There sure are a lot of circles and shaded squares today; a visual assault.

Take HEAVEN ON EARTH. There's the usual challenge of trying to figure out where the spaces are in that phrase, but then you add in the circles. A N O N, okay. Wait. You also have to focus on the shaded squares? That interweave in and out of the circles? It's not my idea of heaven on earth, but as a change of pace, it can work EVER and ANON.

Excellent gridwork, David doing everything right with the layout. Pairs of adjacent long Downs in the lower left and upper right, where they only have to work with one themer apiece. Black squares wisely deployed, focusing on separating the themers, especially in that dense middle.

Now that I've been able to rub my eyes and refocus, I appreciate the finds more. Concentrating to find four that were as tasty as BLACK FOREST HAM would have made for a fantastic 15x15.
