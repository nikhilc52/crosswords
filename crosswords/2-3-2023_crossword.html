This puzzle is personal. It got its start when I faced a scary medical procedure in April, 2021, and thought to myself, "Wish me luck." That was of course followed by the thought that WISH ME LUCK would be a good phrase to put in a crossword. I started a grid at that time, anchored by that phrase.

I came back to the partial grid late in 2021. After a lot of iterations on the grid, and a lot of time writing clues, I had something ready to submit. I'm glad that this puzzle is my first themeless for the New York Times.

While I kind of wish I had been the one to debut WISH ME LUCK, I thoroughly enjoyed Ekua Ewool's puzzle that beat me to it.

So much of a themeless solving experience is personal. I used to have ELKHOUNDS in my life and remember them with great fondness. I'm an ORGAN DONOR who used to TA statistics in my MBA program, trying to get execs to the point where a BELL CURVE would ring a bell. Before that, this mechanical engineer would tell clients who demanded the impossible that all we had to do was make the parts out of UNOBTANIUM.

NOTEWORTHY entries all around, Barbara hitting my sweet spots everywhere I looked.

And what a clean grid — just a bit of INTL LYTE. Such a well-made 70-word themeless!

A few clues could have used a Friday tune-up. Cyclists know that BIKE RIDES can be measured in centuries — 100 mile units — but that's deep, specialized lingo. Whether or not you know what "normal distribution" means, there's no a-ha, simply a dictionary definition to get to BELL CURVE.

Not depending on such specialized knowledge made [Finishing-line cry?] hit the target, making me yell BINGO! as if I'd finished that line on my bingo card.

Given how many entries evoked memories of great times in my life, IF I WERE YOU, I wouldn't give a second thought to why Barbara picked up her third POW!
