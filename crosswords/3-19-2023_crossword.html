Robert Ryan, of London, is an economist for the U.K.'s Competition and Markets Authority. He grew up in Ireland, where he used to sit on his father's knee and "help" him solve the cryptic crossword in the back of a television guide. He subscribed to The New York Times in 2020 for its election coverage and soon got hooked on the crossword, which he now does every day.

This is Robert's first puzzle for the paper.

I don't know Python (yet), so to find words that could potentially be split to invent new two-word phrases, I reverted to Stata, the only coding language I know, thanks to my day job as an economist. It produced quite a lot of potential matches, but most of those had to go in the bin for being uninteresting, clunky, lacking in good symmetrical partners or (in the case of "sacredness") unlikely to pass the ick test no matter how you clue it.

My notes on the puzzle over on Wordplay have a few that were closer to making the submission. My favourite clue that didn't make it was [German for kindergarten, say?] for ETYMOLOGY, but I like the replacement [Word has it!] too. I was glad that [More than just a sec?] survived for BRUT.

One point of trivia that came up in the cluing discussion was whether XANDER was Buffy's best friend. We opted for "friend" out of deference to Willow. But Buffy fans can debate!

A word to the wise — the puzzle actually got accepted in July of 2022, but I was none the wiser for six months, thanks to an overzealous spam filter!

Years ago, Parker Lewis and I tried our hand at single-word parsing. I'd randomly observed that TEMPERAMENTALLY (can you tell I'm a parent of young children?) interestingly breaks into an Arizona noodle census. How hard could it be to find others that broke out like this?

Dozens of lines of code and months of sifting through hundreds of false positives later …

Parker and I were focused solely on breaks into three, so I enjoyed seeing today's with fresh eyes. KIN ESTHETIC reminds me how glad I am that our kids inherited Jill's looks instead of mine, and a DEPARTMENTALLY to DEPART MENTALLY is a joke that writes itself.

Such solid gridwork! Most experienced constructors let about ten gluey dribs and drabs slide. To finish with only BFS and RMN (maybe AGLARE is aglaring to some) is such a smooth result.

Arriving at a Sunday 140-word puzzle with this level of cleanliness means forcing yourself to work at a high minimum word score, rarely letting yourself dip into "look the other way" mode. It's not a difficult process — the difficulty comes in exercising the patience needed to continually backtrack and go down a different branch of the decision tree when you're forced into something unsavory.

I wasn't wowed by enough of the finds, but bonuses like the colloquial IDIOT TAX and ALBUM ART helped keep me going. And as much as I admired Robert's wordplay in his ETYMOLOGY clue, the new one, [Word has it!], is playful as well. Touches like that, plus the awesome [House floor manager?] to describe a ROOMBA, encouraged me to reach the finish line.
