Sid Sivakumar is an M.D./Ph.D. student at Washington University in St. Louis. This is his 15th crossword for The Times in three years.

He likes theme ideas and clues that are challenging but not forced. When you figure something out, he wants you to say, "Of course!," not "Uh ... OK, I get it." When possible, Sid includes South Asian references in his puzzles, as in the clue for 111-Down here.

I spent an hour trying to figure out what was going on, and then another hour coming up with ways to deflect all the incoming questions. ZIPPER MERGES gets at two Down entries heading into one of the five merge spots, at which point, a third Down entry alternates the ends of the first two.

So much for that hour.

Let's try a visual example:

The ends of HOWCANTHATBE and SILVERMEDAL zipper together to form HEADTABLE.

I tried to think of some other way of using visual cues to help this explanation, but the guy in my head kept honking at every idea I had.

Jim Horne and I discussed ways of helping people figure out what was going on, and we landed on the Krakenization seen below. I'm not much of an ice hockey fan, but you gotta love the team making its first playoff and going deep! [The Seahawks, Mariners, Sounders, and Kraken all sport the same color scheme.]

How can you even find pairs that work this way? Well, arrays and dictionary coding constructs allow you to—

HOOOOOONK

I used to love this sort of brain-busting construction — Patrick Berry's simpler implementation blew me away when I first started. I've had so many solvers throw pencils at me over the years, though, that these days, I ease up to be more solver-friendly. I'm ecstatic for those gritty folks who gritted through this one and earned their fantastic a-ha moment — and I'm also sympathetic to those who got too frustrated or even stymied.

Since the Sunday Magazine can employ color, Krakenizing could have been spectacular. Although it would make the concept too simple for some, it would make it much more accessible to a broader audience.
