Hello again, Crossworld! I hope that the gimmick in this puzzle was the X-factor in your solving experience …

I'm a senior at Tufts University, studying Computer Science and Math. I wrote this puzzle in my spare time while interning for the NYT Puzzles team this summer as a software developer; a huge thank-you to everyone there for a wonderful experience.

This puzzle was originally scheduled for a Saturday. However, I'm pleased to make my Thursday debut! Will and the team did a great job with the cluing, but I'm happy to see a couple of my favorites make the cut (I'm particularly fond of 18-Across). Given the low density of the theme, I tried to keep the fill as clean as possible. Other than ENO, I'm pleased with the result. I'm interested to see how the Thursday crowd will react; this puzzle might play more like a themeless than your typical Thursday trickery.

I imagine I'm going to get a lot of confusion-laden emails today, from people who missed the giant black X in the grid — four representations of X: NEW YORK (TIMES), MALCOLM (X), (KISS) GOODBYE, (CROSS) SECTION.

It's not the first time we've seen the idea of using a letter made out of black squares, or a symbol representing a range of words, but John did a fantastic job in executing this one. Four themers that start or end with a 7-letter phrase makes for beautiful symmetry, and all four themers are super-solid. Great decisions.

What was truly standout was the gridwork. This is a 72-word puzzle, qualifying it for themeless territory, so it's already tough to begin with. And with four 7-letter entries locked into place, so much flexibility is taken away. Given that, I would have expected there to be either a lot of gluey bits to hold everything together or for the long slots to be filled with neutral or subpar entries.

Not so! Man oh man was there greatness all around. From SAUTE PAN to HOME BREW to CRIME LAB to HUMANITY to ANECDOTE to IM BATMAN to NERD CRED to MCGREGOR to ALL IN ONE, this would compare favorably to many 72-words themelesses. Stellar work.

Smart decision to leave himself with a ton of 8-letter slots. Some constructors would have favored more symmetry, by breaking up each side 7/7 instead of 8/6. While I can see how that might have been visually more pleasing, I'd much rather have all the snazzy fill that the 8-letter slots opened up.

Very well done. I gave the puzzle serious POW! consideration based on superb execution alone.
