Alex Eaton-Salners, of Lake Forest, Calif., works as an in-house attorney for a technology company.

In addition to making crosswords, he is a prolific variety puzzle-maker for the Times, including the Puns and Anagrams found in this issue (solve, solution, print, collaborate) He also invents new puzzle types, such as Hop Cross Fun (print, solution), which ran in the Magazine on 9/15.

"I like to stretch my limits with different constructing experiences," he says.

There's a lot going on here, but the trick is explained in the title.

"Shape Up" means that the four vertical circles reading upward spell shapes (cone, star, oval, and cross) that get appended to horizontal answers: LEMON SCONE, BASKETBALL STAR, SEAL OF APPROVAL, and COMES ACROSS.

"Ship Out" means the four horizontal ships (or at least water vessels) are eliminated to match the clues: BEEPER, DUB, SIN, and TRACTS.

Note that "Nickname" at 64-Across is a verb.

Fun fact: SCUMBAG (used only once) is no longer considered acceptable at the NYT. Yet, 110-Across reads as COMESAC.

Fun fact 2: The answer RINO has been used twice before. In 1983, the clue was "Epicarp". Didn't know that one? Perhaps the 1952 clue "Money: slang" would be easier for you.
