Jeremy Newton, of Austin, Tex., is an engineering manager for mobile games at SciPlay. He's been making crosswords for The Times since 2008.

He says this one is a special accomplishment, because the idea for it has been kicking around since 2010. He finally managed to find a consistent theme set he was happy with. While creating the puzzle he mumbled a lot. You'll soon see why.

Jeremy mixing things up today! It took me forever to sound these out, and when I finally made the leap from THEIR RITES TOUGH to THE RIGHT STUFF, I wondered, why this movie? Okay, it was an OSCAR WINNER, but aren't there hundreds of Oscar winners, and what do these phonetic simulacrums have to do with—

They all won Oscars for SOUND MIXING!

The award goes to Jeremy for such a brilliant set of connections. It all seemed so random, but when I hit that revealer, I knew whose name would be in the envelope.

Themes with nonsensical strings of words force solvers to only use the Downs. While this is a strategy employed by speed-solvers looking to improve their times, it can make for a frustrating experience for us mere mortals. Even after I had pieced together THAITANKNICK letter by painstaking letter, I had to double and triple-check every crossing, because THAIT TANK looked so ICK.

Funny image, Jeremy mumbling to himself as he sounded out hundreds of possibilities. I wonder if his wife or mine looked more worried, as he created or as I mumbled my way through all pronunciations.

Several debut entries, ALIEN LIFE and BACKSLASH solid. Some of the others work as neutral fill — SITTING IN, HEAD ON IN, NODS OUT aren't going to win any prizes — but I paused at WALL HUNG and DO A DUET. Although both are real phrases, what with all the frustration of piecing the themers together, I wanted more familiar surrounding fill to help me get toeholds.

This is the type of out-of-the-box thinking that leads to a standout Sunday crossword. Such leaps, from movie titles to Oscar winners to Oscar winners to SOUND MIXING? Beautiful mix of concepts.
