John Ewbank is a British science writer from Macclesfield, near Manchester. He regularly composes cryptic crosswords for The Times of London, but lately has been trying American-style crosswords.

This is his third for us. The theme is examples of anapodoton (an-uh-POH-duh-tahn), a rhetorical device you probably use yourself but never knew there was a name for. Well, now you'll know.

The technical term for these kinds of phrases is "anapodoton," defined as when the main clause of a phrase ("Do as the Romans do") is not stated but implied by a sub-clause ("When in Rome…."). It can also be used in threats ("If you do that one more time…) or statements ("If you think I'm going to stand for your insults…).

Wikipedia tells me that it's common in classical Chinese; for example, "A frog in a well…" is short for "A frog in a well cannot conceive of the ocean," a saying by philosopher Zhuangzi which essentially means that you should get out more. Every day's a school day, even Sunday—end lecture.

This was my first attempt at a Sunday puzzle — it was frankly terrifying, and at times I despaired of finding a fill. But where there's a will…

Several years back, I spent many hours mulling over phrases like A BIRD IN THE HAND, WHEN THE GOING GETS TOUGH, IF THE SHOE FITS … How to score them for our word list? Weighing their partialish feel vs. their evocative implications gave me fits. Ultimately, I decided that IF YOU HAVE TO ASK …

(Yeah, I still don't have a great answer.)

Thankfully, I didn't have to ask about John's gridwork. Having had the pleasure of working with him, I knew the strength of his technical skills. It surprised me that he had qualms about working on a Sunday, though most all constructors are hesitant to tackle the 140-word 21x21 challenge.

What gave me no surprise at all was that John produced an almost perfectly-smooth grid with a couple of great bonuses. To be sure, ENERGY CZAR, SECRET MENU, DALAI LAMA, LASER TAG, TO BE SURE are excellent. With hardly a short entry to speed bump my solve, it's exactly the kind of grid I desire.

Birds of a feather …

A friend and I used to make up these kinds of semi-complete phrases to annoy an overly-inquisitive Ultimate Frisbee teammate. "The person who blinks first …" followed by a suggestive eyebrow raise did wonders.

For our entertainment, that is. Not for his sanity.

I'm not a fan of listicle-like puzzles, so a random assortment of these phrase types didn't hit me strongly. But you know what they say: Every day you highlight a Greek word …
