This is another satisfying case of a theme idea that was collecting dust until I finally found that last piece to make it all work. I had the revealer HIDDEN AGENDA and the two shorter themers for quite some time. Then one day I came upon VOLKSWAGEN DASHER, the last puzzle piece! I think nowadays the editors aren't too excited by a theme with the same exact word hidden in each themer, but I guess it's a good find when it's a six-letter word.

I had to stretch the grid width to 16 blocks and split the revealer into two parts, but we made it work! Very helpful that the two shorter themers were both 10 letters.

Something I noticed that I thought was interesting is that all three revealers include foreign (or fake) words. To find three phrases with words ending in AGEN, we needed a Hebrew word (MAGEN), a German word (VOLKSWAGEN), and a Danish-sounding word (HAAGEN).

Anyway, I hope you enjoy. I haven't had a daily in the NYT in almost three years! If you like wordplay, you can check out my cryptics and puns & anagrams, which have been coming out more often.

HÄAGEN DAZS and VOLKSWAGEN … brands with umlauts! "Name That Theme," defeated!

Wait. MAGEN DAVID? Unlikely to have an umlaut.

And now I realize that VOLKSWAGEN also doesn't have an umlaut.

The HIDDEN / AGENDA is easily "make fun of Jeff's stupidity."

What isn't easy is finding such a long hidden word, split across words of a phrase. I'd seen AGENDA inside HAAGEN DAZS before, and I was vaguely familiar with the term MAGEN DAVID, but I would never have guessed that three hidden AGENDAs were possible. Creative find in VOLKSWAGEN DASHER. I appreciated that the clue nudged to the correct answer since that car dashed into the night decades ago.

Some tough vocab for an early-week puzzle — my newb friends will be texting me. ARRANT seems like a pirate's version of ERRANT, ENNEAD's prefix is much less common than hexa- or octa- or deca-, and I recognize KETONE from chem college classes but would have no idea how to define it.

Any one of them might be fine on its own. All three being six letters long … 666 … could it be ...

Puzzles full of proper nouns can be divisive. PENZANCE will elate some, confuse others. Throw in upwards of 20 others, and it's the most proper-heavy property in recent memory.

"Hidden words" themes are a tough sell these days, especially when you repeat the same word over and over again. However, embedding such a long target word helped.
